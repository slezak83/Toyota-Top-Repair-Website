2017 Tacoma D4S Injection Broken Wiring

This Tacoma came in with multiple codes for a cylinder number two and cylinder six misfire on top of an open direct injection injector for number two and six. I then proceeded to the ecm to test the wiring and found the wiring back to the injectors were open. Once I removed the upper intake plenum I found what confirmed exactly what had happened. Mice had chewed up the wiring in the injector sub-harness underneath the intake plenum. (See image above and below)
If you are going to diagnose this fast I suggest to start from the GRAY and BLACK six pin connectors (six pins inside the connector but has room for 8 pins[gray] and 10 pins[black]) for the D4S system directly underneath the throttlebody. If it's not open there, then obviously it's further back to the ecm and if not, then probably you have a wiring issue or an injector issue. To get to the ecm is difficult because it's located directly behind the glovebox.

Restricted Fuel Injectors or Carbon?

I really wished I had better information based upon personal experience. My experience with this problem started with p0171 on a 2az-fe engine, but it was what I noticed in the techstream data that really got my attention. When you use techstream create a custom data list (refresh rate is faster) and select fuel trim data (short term and long term bank 1 and 2) and Mass Airflow. Hook up a fuel pressure gauge so that you do not miss low fuel pressure if it drops! Take a look below...

The key here is that when this car was idling the fuel trim was steady at 7% long term and relatively low short term (near +/-zero). The fuel pressure stayed constant at 47psi. I then put the car in drive with my foot on the brakes revved and watched the MAF sensor shoot up to 47 grams/sec. The fuel trim seemed to become erratic so I watched what happened on decel during fuel cut and did not notice it getting very lean or misfiring. Usually during fuel cut during decel you get misfiring from carbon build up or a valve not closing. If ignition was weak it wouldn't burn everything and the exhaust stream would become rich and might misfire during acceleration. I revved to 2000 rpm and held it while watching the fuel trims and found that when I did this, the long term went up to +21.00% and +15.00% short term. I knew now this could only be a fuel injection problem. Scroll down and look what happened after I replaced the fuel injectors.

In the picture above you can see exactly what should happen when you have fixed the problem. In the picture above the short term fuel trim starts to over correct for the previous learned values for the lean condition. It now goes -18.00% on one bank and -14.88% on the other bank of this 2az-fe. I have seen this two times since then on the same engine with the same symptoms. I am coming to the conclusion that this is a typical restricted fuel injector. So there you have it! If you find these symptoms after checking all the basics in your repair manual, then try this as one of your next tests. Beware that Siennas have been getting this lean code from a weak fuel pump. Don't try to replace a set of six injectors if all you have is bad fuel pressure. CHECK THE BASICS FIRST!!!

Torque Converter Shudder

This shudder happened in a RAV4 but the diagnosis remains the exact same with a u660E transmission. The trick I have found is to get a data list of the highlighted data you see in the picture below and you need to understand the difference between SLU and Lock-up parameters. The difference is Lock-up is the converter in full lock-up over 50MPH and SLU is the flex lock-up which is a duty-cycled (pulsed on and off) torque converter clutch solenoid parameter. The shudder found in TSB's is only occurring during flex lock-up, therefore, duplicate the shudder at 25-35mph when flex lock (SLU) turns ON.
Start recording the test drive. Everytime you feel the shudder, hit the space bar to flag it. When you review your data it should look like the picture above. If you get to this point you should review my tech tips page first before replacing.

Diagnosing Misfire From Carbon Build-up

In the cases of very old Toyota engines with high mileage and a pretty severe lack of maintenance, or in the cases of the newer Yaris motors, carbon is going to become an issue. Toyota has been designing lower tension oil control rings and if you watch the humble mechanic on YouTube, you'll find out why this is such a big issue here. The basics of it are this in my opinion: The engine is shut off and the presence of unburned fuel vapors condenses in a still humid environment and fuel droplets are pulled out of the air and settle on top of pistons and at the end of the injectors. After the engine cools the fuel eventually turns into a sticky varnish and upon the next cold start an excessive amount of oil blows by the rings because they have low tension and gathers on the fuel varnish, gets hot and turns into carbon.
If you diagnose this, my tip for you is to find out how you can track a misfire without the presence of fuel or spark. You can't very well do it by disabling any injectors with techstream or just doing a simple compression test because those won't be enough and sometimes that stuff is time consuming. What I do is monitor only the misfire counter for each cylinder, the fuel trims, and fuel cut data parameters while recording my entire test drive to watch later. I then get the car up to highway speeds and decelerate and see if when I replay my test drive, if the misfire happened at the same time as the fuel cut. If your fuel trim gets extremely positive, the carbon inside the motor is actually soaking the fuel causing a slightly lean condition. MAF will possibly also drop during acceleration before you decel. This is not foolproof but it is quick and easy to do while checking the vehicle for any pulls on a test drive.
